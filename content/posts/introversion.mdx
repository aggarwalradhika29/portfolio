Tech has a visibility problem — or rather, it has a *loudness* problem. The people who get noticed are often the ones who talk the most in meetings, post the most on LinkedIn, and have opinions about everything in real time.

As an introvert, I spent a long time thinking this meant I was doing something wrong.

## What introversion actually is

I want to clear something up, because there's a lot of noise around this word. Introversion isn't shyness. It isn't anxiety. It isn't a reluctance to engage with people.

It's about energy. Social interaction costs me something. I need quiet time to process, to think, to recover. After a long day of meetings, I don't want to go out — I want to be alone with a book or a walk or a meal I cook slowly.

This is not a flaw. It's a cognitive style.

## The introvert's advantage

Here's what I've noticed about how I work:

I think before I speak, which means when I do say something, it tends to be considered. I don't need external validation to stay motivated on a project — I can go deep alone for hours. I notice things in conversations that louder people sometimes miss because they're busy formulating their next point.

In data engineering specifically, this has been genuinely useful. The work rewards patience. It rewards the willingness to sit with a problem until you actually understand it, rather than reaching for the first solution that presents itself. It rewards reading documentation carefully, tracing data lineage precisely, noticing when something is subtly off.

None of these are loudness skills.

## The parts that are genuinely hard

I won't pretend there are no costs. There are.

Visibility in tech often comes from how much you perform your work — talks, posts, presence. This does not come naturally to me. I have to actively resist the tendency to just do the work quietly and assume it will speak for itself, because sometimes it doesn't.

Meetings are draining in a way that they simply aren't for extroverts. A day heavy with meetings leaves me with very little left for deep work, and I've had to learn to protect my calendar accordingly.

And there's still a bias — subtle but real — toward the people who speak up, take up space, project confidence loudly. Learning to show up in my own register, without mimicking a style that isn't mine, has been an ongoing project.

## What I've stopped doing

I've stopped apologising for needing quiet. I've stopped performing enthusiasm I don't feel. I've stopped equating visibility with value.

And I've started to understand that the industry needs both kinds of minds — the ones who generate energy in a room, and the ones who can hold a complex problem alone for three hours without getting bored.

I'm the second kind. That's not a consolation prize.
