There's a particular kind of exhaustion that comes not from doing too much, but from *believing* you should be doing more.

I spent a long time in that place. Evenings that should have been rest turned into side projects. Weekends that wanted to be quiet became opportunities to catch up. The metric of a good day was how much I had done — not how present I had been in any of it.

I don't think this is unusual for people in tech. We live in an industry that has aestheticised busyness. Productivity systems have entire communities. There are subreddits dedicated to optimising sleep so you can work more. It becomes very easy to internalise the idea that your value is proportional to your output.

## The moment something shifted

I can't point to a single thing. It was more like a slow accumulation of small noticings.

I noticed that my most interesting conversations were the ones that went nowhere useful — that wandered through ideas without agenda. I noticed that the books I remembered most were the ones I read slowly, without trying to extract lessons. I noticed that the weeks I felt most like myself were the ones with the most empty space in them.

Slowness, I started to realise, wasn't the absence of productivity. It was a different relationship with time — one where not everything needed to justify its existence.

## What it actually looks like

I want to be honest that choosing slowness doesn't mean doing nothing. I still work hard. I still build things. But I've started to be more deliberate about what gets my full attention and what doesn't.

It looks like: cooking dinner without a podcast on. Walking without tracking steps. Reading without taking notes. Having a weekend day with no plans and resisting the urge to fill it.

It looks like letting a thought sit unfinished, and trusting that it will either resolve itself or reveal that it didn't matter that much.

## The thing nobody says

The thing nobody tells you is that slowness is where most of the good thinking happens. The ideas that have actually changed how I work — about data systems, about how to structure a problem — rarely came from focused effort. They came from the shower, from a walk, from half-reading something unrelated.

The brain needs unstructured time the way a field needs to lie fallow. You can extract from it constantly, but eventually you get diminishing returns.

I'm still learning this. Some weeks I slip back into the optimisation mindset and don't notice until I'm tired in a way that sleep doesn't fix. But I'm getting better at recognising it — and at giving myself permission to stop.

Slowness isn't a reward for when the work is done. It's part of how the work gets done well.
