## on designing hard problems for language models

for about four weeks in september ’25, part of my work involved designing problems that were meant to be hard for large language models.

not just typical prompts — genuinely difficult ones.

np-hard tasks, tricky programming problems, quantitative questions with many constraints, reasoning chains where you had to keep track of several steps at once. the goal was to build datasets that could test whether models were actually reasoning, or just producing answers that sounded right.

it ended up being one of the most interesting things i’ve worked on.

the process itself was simple.

design the problem.
solve it myself to make sure it was actually solvable.
document the reasoning.
and then evaluate the model’s answer.

that last part was where things got interesting.

because language models are extremely good at sounding correct.

the explanation is structured well.
the language is confident.
the steps look logical.

and then you check the final answer — and it’s wrong.

sometimes subtly wrong. sometimes completely off.

after a while i started noticing something: the failures weren’t random.

models tend to take the path of least resistance through a problem. they pattern-match to something similar instead of reasoning through every constraint. if the task requires holding too many conditions in working memory, they often lose track of one somewhere in the middle.

in a strange way, it’s a very human kind of mistake.

the difference is that humans usually pause when they feel unsure. models don’t — they continue confidently.

the hardest problems to design were the ones that looked easy but weren’t. questions where the obvious approach leads you confidently in the wrong direction. those were the most revealing, because they separated models that were reasoning from models that were just performing reasoning.

after a few weeks of this, my mental model of ai shifted a little.

these systems are incredibly good at synthesising knowledge across domains. ask them to connect ideas from many places, and they’re impressive.

but ask them to execute a careful multi-step procedure without making a mistake, and things get less reliable.

that doesn’t make the technology less remarkable. if anything, it makes it more interesting.

i don't have a clean conclusion from this. but it's shaped how i think about where ai augments human work, versus where it needs to be watched carefully.
