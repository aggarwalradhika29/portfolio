Most of the narratives we have about growth are retrospective. Someone looks back from a place of clarity and traces the path that led them there. The struggle is real in the telling, but it's been made coherent by hindsight.

What nobody really talks about is what it feels like *while you're in it* — before you know what it's becoming, before the story has a shape.

## The middle of things

I've been in the middle of something for a while now.

I'm good at my work. I enjoy it. And at the same time, I'm aware of a reaching — toward something I can't quite articulate. More ownership, maybe. More creative agency. Building things that are mine, not just contributing to things that belong to someone else.

This isn't dissatisfaction exactly. It's more like the feeling of a word on the tip of your tongue. You know there's something there. You can sense its edges. But it hasn't resolved into clarity yet.

## Why becoming feels uncomfortable

I think we expect growth to feel good — like levelling up, like adding. But a lot of growth feels more like subtraction first. Shedding versions of yourself that were comfortable. Sitting with uncertainty about who the next version is.

The uncomfortable part isn't that things are hard. It's that they're *unclear*. Hard things with clear paths are actually fine — you just do the work. The genuinely difficult thing is not knowing which direction the work should go.

## What I'm learning to do with it

I'm learning to treat the uncertainty as information rather than a problem. The reaching means something is alive in me that wants more room. That's not a bad thing, even when it's uncomfortable.

I'm learning to be a fair witness to where I actually am — not ahead of myself, not behind. Just here, in the middle, doing the next thing that seems true.

I'm also learning that most people who look like they have it figured out are also mid-journey. They've just gotten better at carrying it quietly. This is oddly comforting.

## A note on patience

I'm not a naturally patient person. I like to understand things, to solve things, to move. Sitting with open questions doesn't come easily.

But I've noticed that the things I've rushed toward clarity on have often been the things I've had to undo later. The shape of something important usually takes the time it needs.

So for now, I'm trying to stay curious about where this goes — and to trust that the not-knowing is part of the knowing.

The middle of a becoming is still a becoming.
